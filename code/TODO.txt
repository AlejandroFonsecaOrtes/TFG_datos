## Task A: Compare the Leading SPOD Mode with Phase-Averaged Fields

**Context & Objective (The "Why"):**
Phase-averaging and SPOD isolate coherent structures through fundamentally different mathematical principles. Phase-averaging works in the time domain: it requires a trigger signal to align snapshots at the same cycle phase and takes their arithmetic mean, yielding a spatial field for each of the 20 phase bins. SPOD works in the frequency domain: it decomposes the cross-spectral density tensor at each frequency into orthogonal spatial modes ranked by energy, without any trigger.

Despite these radically different approaches, if the jet forcing is truly monochromatic (single dominant frequency $f_0$), the leading SPOD mode at $f_0$ should capture the same dominant spatial vortex structure as the phase-average. Mathematically, the phase-average at phase $\phi$ can be written as a Fourier series over multiples of $f_0$. The first harmonic dominates, and its spatial shape should be exactly the leading SPOD mode at $f_0$.

Demonstrating this equivalence serves two purposes: (1) it physically validates the SPOD computation in this project, and (2) it shows that SPOD can extract phase-coherent structures without external triggering—the same conclusion as Task B below, but from a spectral rather than clustering perspective.

**Implementation Steps:**
1. **Load SPOD results:** Load pre-computed SPOD from `SPOD_data/RUN2_PIV_SPOD.npz`. Extract `freqs` $(129,)$, `eigvals` $(129, 14)$, and `eigvecs` $(129, 14, 171622)$.
2. **Identify forcing frequency:** From `data/info.txt`, RUN2 has $St_{act} = 0.05$. Locate the frequency bin $f_k$ in `freqs` closest to this value. Extract $\Psi_1(f_k) \in \mathbb{C}^{171622}$—the leading SPOD eigenvector at that frequency.
3. **Extract spatial structure:** Split the complex mode into its $u$ and $v$ components (first $269 \times 319$ entries = $u$, last $269 \times 319$ = $v$). The real part $\Re(\Psi_1)$ represents the mode shape at phase $0$; the imaginary part $\Im(\Psi_1)$ at phase $\pi/2$. Take the magnitude $|\Psi_1|$ for an amplitude map.
4. **Load and prepare phase-averages:** From the `.mat` file (`data/Run2_PIV.mat`), extract `Uph`, `Vph` $(20, N_x N_y)$ and subtract the mean (`Um`, `Vm`). Reshape each phase into $(269, 319)$ for both components.
5. **Compare:** Compute cosine similarity between $\Re(\Psi_1)$ (or $|\Psi_1|$) and each phase-averaged fluctuating field. Plot side-by-side contour maps. Use `plot_spod_mode()` from `data_utils.py` for the SPOD mode and `plot_velocity_field()` for the phase-averages.

## Task B: Assign Clusters to Phase-Averaged Fields & Verify Physical Correspondence

**Context & Objective (The "Why"):**
In forced jet experiments, a phase-averaged flow field is computed by triggering image acquisition at a known phase of the actuation cycle. This requires dedicated hardware (e.g., a microphone or TTL trigger synchronized with the actuator). The result is a set of 20 phase-averaged velocity templates $P_i$ ($i = 1, \dots, 20$), each representing the expected spatial structure at a specific moment within one actuation period.

Separately, unsupervised clustering has been applied to the SPCA temporal scores (see `PCA.ipynb`), grouping snapshots into $K$ clusters without any knowledge of the actuation phase. Each cluster centroid, when projected back to the physical velocity space, represents the "average flow pattern" of that group.

The question this task answers is: **Do the unsupervised clusters correspond to specific phases of the actuation cycle?** If cluster centroid $C_k$ is geometrically close to phase-average $P_i$, it means the algorithm has "blindly" recovered the deterministic, phase-locked vortex structures without any trigger information. This validates the Manifold Hypothesis—namely, that the dominant flow dynamics live on a low-dimensional cyclical manifold parameterized by the actuation phase.

**Implementation Steps:**
1. **Load cluster centroids:** Load pre-computed cluster labels from `clustering_data/RUN2_PIV_labels.npz` (`labels` array, shape $(2030,)$). Load `u`, `v` from `compressed_data/RUN2_PIV_compressed.npz`. Compute cluster centroids by averaging the velocity fields of all snapshots assigned to each cluster (see the `compute_cluster_centroids` function in `PCA.ipynb` cell [80] for reference—reimplement it locally in your notebook).
2. **Load phase-averaged fields:** Use `data_utils.load_piv_data()` on `data/Run2_PIV.mat` to extract `Uph` and `Vph` (shape $(20, N_x \times N_y)$). Also extract `Um` and `Vm` (time-mean fields, shape $(269, 319)$). Compute the fluctuating phase-averages: $P_i = (U_{ph,i} - U_m, V_{ph,i} - V_m)$, then flatten and concatenate to match the centroid format.
3. **Compute cosine similarity:** For every cluster centroid $C_k \in \mathbb{R}^{2 N_x N_y}$ and every fluctuating phase-average $P_i \in \mathbb{R}^{2 N_x N_y}$, compute:
   $$\text{sim}(C_k, P_i) = \frac{C_k \cdot P_i}{\|C_k\| \, \|P_i\|}$$
   This produces a similarity matrix of shape $(K, 20)$.
4. **Assign and evaluate:** For each cluster, find the phase with maximum similarity. Report the assignment table and the average best-match similarity. Visualize side-by-side the centroid and its best-matching phase-average using `plot_velocity_field()` from `data_utils.py`.

## Task A: Apply t-SNE to Standard PCA Scores

**Context & Objective (The "Why"):**
The current pipeline (in `PCA.ipynb`) applies t-SNE to SPCA scores, which works well but involves sparsity regularization. Applying t-SNE directly to standard PCA scores (without sparsity) provides a complementary view: it reveals the manifold geometry as captured by the energy-optimal linear basis.

PCA is a linear projection. It captures variance efficiently but cannot represent non-linear topology—a periodic trajectory through flow states (phase 1 → 2 → ... → 20 → 1) would appear as an ellipse or a linear spread in PCA space, rather than a closed loop. t-SNE preserves local neighborhoods non-linearly, so it can "unwrap" the cyclical structure into a visually interpretable 2D embedding.

If the t-SNE embedding of PCA scores shows a continuous, circularly ordered loop (and coloring by phase reveals a smooth gradient around the loop), it confirms that the dominant flow dynamics trace a one-dimensional closed orbit in the high-dimensional state space. This is direct visual evidence for the Manifold Hypothesis.

Comparing this PCA-based embedding with the existing SPCA-based one (in `PCA.ipynb`) reveals whether sparsity regularization alters the perceived manifold topology.

**Implementation Steps:**
1. **Compute PCA scores:** Load `u`, `v` from `compressed_data/RUN2_PIV_compressed.npz`. Flatten to a snapshot matrix $(2030, 171622)$. Apply `sklearn.decomposition.PCA`. Retain the first $k$ components capturing ~90% variance.
2. **Run t-SNE:** Use `sklearn.manifold.TSNE` with `n_components=2`. Test multiple perplexity values (e.g., 30, 50, 100). Run multiple random seeds and select the embedding with lowest KL divergence (the `run_multiple_tsne` function in `PCA.ipynb` cell [86] implements this pattern — reimplement locally).
3. **Visualize:** Scatter plot the 2D embedding. Color by snapshot time-index to check for temporal continuity.

## Task B: Frequency Spectrum of Sparse PCA Temporal Coefficients

**Context & Objective (The "Why"):**
Standard PCA modes are "global"—a single spatial mode contains non-zero values across the entire domain. Sparse PCA forces spatial localization by adding an L1 penalty, producing modes where only a subset of spatial points are active. This makes them physically more interpretable: each mode highlights a localized flow region.

The temporal coefficient $z_k(t)$ associated with each sparse mode records how "active" that localized structure is at each time step. Its Power Spectral Density (PSD) reveals whether the localized structure oscillates at a specific frequency or behaves chaotically:

- **Sharp peak at $f_0$ (the forcing frequency):** The localized region is dominated by the deterministic actuation. The sparse mode has isolated a forced vortex.
- **Broadband, flat spectrum:** The localized region is dominated by incoherent turbulence. The sparse mode has isolated random fluctuations.
- **Peaks at harmonics/subharmonics:** Non-linear interactions in the jet shear layer.

This spectral characterization shows that sparsity-based decomposition can spatially and spectrally disentangle deterministic forcing from stochastic turbulence—something standard PCA cannot do because its global modes mix multiple frequency contributions.

**Implementation Steps:**
1. **Load pre-computed SPCA results:** Load `SPCA_data/RUN2_PIV_SPCA.npz`. Extract `scores` $(2030, 30)$ and `components` $(30, 171622)$.
2. **Compute PSD:** For each of the 30 temporal coefficient columns in `scores`, apply `scipy.signal.welch(scores[:, k], fs=1.0, nperseg=256)` (matching the SPOD block size for consistency).
3. **Visualization:** Plot the PSDs. Overlay a vertical dashed line at the known forcing frequency $St_{act} = 0.05$ for RUN2. Group modes by whether they show tonal or broadband behavior.
4. **Spatial context:** For the modes that peak at $f_0$, plot their spatial structure (reshape `components[k]` into $(2, 269, 319)$ for the $u$ and $v$ parts) to show which physical region the mode occupies.

## Task C: Project t-SNE to 1 Dimension and Order by Phase

**Context & Objective (The "Why"):**
The Intrinsic Dimension analysis (`intrinsic_dimension.ipynb`) estimated that the jet-flow manifold has a dimension of approximately $d \approx 2\text{–}4$ depending on the estimator. However, if the actuation is perfectly periodic and the turbulent noise is filtered out (via PCA denoising), the dominant dynamics should trace a single closed orbit—a 1D loop parameterized by the actuation phase $\phi \in [0, 2\pi)$.

Projecting the data to exactly 1 dimension with t-SNE tests this hypothesis directly. If successful, the single output coordinate should unroll the cyclical manifold into a monotonic (or piecewise monotonic) function of phase. Plotting the 1D embedding against the known phase index should reveal a sawtooth-like pattern.

This is the most stringent test of the 1D cyclical manifold hypothesis: not just that the data "looks circular" in a 2D plot (Task A), but that a single coordinate suffices to parameterize the entire large-scale dynamics.
